March 2024. I don't pay much attention to Ceratina in western Oregon. They are hyperabundant. They are almost always one species, C. acantha. Rarely, I see one of the other local species (micheneri, naniula). When I collected the Ceratina below a year ago, I noticed at the time that it was exceptionally big. Sorting specimens recently, I noticed the size again, so looked closely. Ceratina tejonensis. Northernmost record for this species, to my knowledge. One always wonders these days ... climate change?

Early queen. I picked up this specimen on a sunny day in February. It is a sweat bee, Lasioglossum. Tiny green ones like this are in subgenus dialictus. It keyed out to L. incompletum, and it matched others I have of this species. Problem: too big - more than 6mm. But an expert confirmed the ID as L. incompletum. Turns out that mine is likely a queen - thus bigger.

Abbreviated process notes:
  1. Wash with detergent, rinse.
  2. Blow-dry, hair dryer on low.
  3. Postion legs, wings, and head on foam with pins. Lots of pins.
  4. Glue on any parts you broke off. Brush out dust and debris.
  5. Dry on a car dashboard in the sun.
  6. Shoot 20-40 images on a WeMacro rail with Helicon Remote.
    • Lens: 65mm Canon macro, or 10X microscope objective on a 70-200mm zoom.
    • Settings: 160th, f5.6.
    • Light: 2 diffused godox MF12 flashes.
  7. Stack images with Helicon software.
  8. Edit in Photoshop. Remove background using eraser over a solid black layer. Standard brightness/contrast adjustments (curves). Spot-heal dust etc.
